# What is Generative AI?

Generative AI refers to artificial intelligence systems that can create new content such as text, images, audio, video, or code. Unlike models that only classify or predict a label, generative models produce outputs that look like they could have been made by humans or by real-world processes.

At a high level, generative AI learns patterns from large datasets and then uses those patterns to produce new examples. For instance, a text-generating model learns how words and sentences typically fit together. When you ask a question or give a prompt, the model predicts what tokens (words or pieces of words) should come next, repeatedly, until it produces a complete response. Image-generating systems learn visual patterns and can synthesize new images that match a prompt or style.

A key idea is that generative AI does not simply “copy and paste” from its training data in normal use. Instead, it learns statistical relationships that allow it to combine concepts in new ways. However, because it is trained on examples, it can sometimes reproduce or closely mimic patterns it has seen before, especially if prompts are very specific or if the training data contains repeated content.

Generative AI can be built using different model families. Large language models generate text and can support tasks such as summarization, tutoring, translation, and drafting. Diffusion models and other image generators can create or edit images. Some systems combine multiple modalities, allowing text to influence images or vice versa.

Generative AI can be useful because it reduces the effort needed to create drafts, explore ideas, and automate repetitive content tasks. In education, it can provide explanations, examples, or practice questions. In design, it can generate variations quickly. In software development, it can suggest code snippets or help document behavior.

There are also limitations and risks. Generative AI can produce plausible-sounding but incorrect information. It can reflect biases present in training data. It may generate content that is inappropriate or unsafe if not constrained. For many applications, it is important to combine generative AI with guardrails: clear prompts, restricted sources, verification, and human oversight.

In short, generative AI is about models that produce new content from learned patterns, enabling creative and practical applications while requiring careful control and validation.
