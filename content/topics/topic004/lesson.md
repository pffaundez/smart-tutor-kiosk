Recommendation systems help platforms decide what content to show you.

They are used in services like Netflix, Spotify, YouTube, and online stores. Their goal is to suggest items you are likely to enjoy or find useful.

There are two main approaches. The first is called content-based filtering. This method looks at what you liked before and recommends similar items. For example, if you watch many action movies, the system will suggest more action movies.

The second approach is collaborative filtering. This method compares your behavior with other users. If people with similar tastes liked something that you have not seen yet, the system may recommend it to you.

Many modern systems combine both approaches. They also use additional signals such as time spent watching, clicks, ratings, and even how quickly you skip content.

Recommendation systems do not truly understand your preferences. They detect patterns in data. Their performance depends on the quality and quantity of user interactions.

These systems are powerful, but they can also create “filter bubbles,” where you are mostly exposed to similar content and fewer new perspectives.
