Supervised learning and unsupervised learning are two common ways that machine learning systems learn from data. The key difference is whether the training data includes labels (correct answers). In supervised learning, each example includes both an input and the desired output, so the model learns to predict a specific target by reducing prediction error over time.

In unsupervised learning, the data has inputs but no labels. Instead of predicting a known answer, the model looks for structure in the data, such as groups of similar items or simpler representations that keep the most important information. Common unsupervised tasks include clustering, dimensionality reduction, and anomaly detection. In practice, you choose supervised learning when you have reliable labels and a clear prediction goal, and you choose unsupervised learning when labels are missing and you want to discover patterns or insights.
Sometimes a related strategy is used called self-supervised learning, where the model generates its own training signals from the data instead of relying on external labels.
